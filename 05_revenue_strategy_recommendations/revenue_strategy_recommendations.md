# Revenue Management Strategy Recommendations

## Purpose of This Section

This section summarizes the main business conclusions from the hotel Revenue Management case study and translates the analytical findings into practical recommendations.

The goal is not only to present data results, but to explain how those results could support real Revenue Management decisions related to segmentation, cancellations, demand forecasting, pricing and inventory control.

---

## 1. Segment Strategy

### Business Insight

The segment performance analysis shows that not all segments contribute to revenue in the same way. Some segments may generate strong room night volume, while others may generate higher ADR or stronger revenue value.

This means that Revenue Management decisions should not be based only on occupancy or booking volume. A segment that fills many rooms is not always the most profitable if its ADR or revenue contribution is weaker.

### Revenue Management Recommendation

The hotel should evaluate segments based on a combination of:

- Room nights contribution
- Revenue contribution
- ADR
- Cancellation behavior
- Strategic value

High-value segments should be prioritized during periods of strong demand, while lower-yield segments should be managed more carefully when inventory is limited.

### Practical RM Action

During high-demand periods, the hotel could protect availability for stronger-value segments and avoid giving too much inventory too early to lower-yield business.

---

## 2. Channel and Cancellation Risk Strategy

### Business Insight

Cancellation behavior can have a direct impact on net revenue, forecast accuracy and inventory availability.

Bookings with higher cancellation risk can create a false sense of demand. If the hotel forecasts only based on gross bookings without considering cancellations, it may overestimate real expected occupancy.

### Revenue Management Recommendation

Cancellation analysis should be used to support:

- Net demand forecasting
- Overbooking decisions
- Channel reliability evaluation
- Cancellation policy review
- Inventory controls

Segments or channels with higher cancellation risk should be monitored separately from more reliable demand sources.

### Practical RM Action

The hotel could apply different overbooking assumptions depending on the cancellation behavior of each segment or channel.

For example, a channel with high cancellation risk may require a stronger overbooking buffer, while a more reliable segment may require less adjustment.

---

## 3. Demand Forecasting Strategy

### Business Insight

Demand changes by month, season and customer segment. A general forecast may hide important differences between segments.

Forecasting demand by segment allows the hotel to understand not only how much demand is expected, but also what type of demand is expected.

### Revenue Management Recommendation

The hotel should use demand forecasting to support:

- Pricing decisions
- Inventory protection
- Segment restrictions
- Commercial planning
- High-demand date identification

Forecasting should not be treated as a purely technical exercise. Its value comes from connecting expected demand with specific business decisions.

### Practical RM Action

If forecasted demand is strong for a specific month or segment, the hotel could increase rates earlier, reduce discounts or protect inventory from lower-value segments.

If forecasted demand is weak, the hotel could open more flexible offers, stimulate direct demand or review channel strategy.

---

## 4. Pricing and Price Elasticity Strategy

### Business Insight

Price elasticity analysis helps evaluate how demand responds to ADR changes.

However, hotel pricing should not be interpreted only through a simple relationship between price and demand. Seasonality, segment mix, booking window, channel behavior and market conditions can all affect the results.

A positive or weak elasticity result may indicate that price is reacting to demand, rather than demand being directly caused by price changes.

### Revenue Management Recommendation

The hotel should avoid using one single pricing rule for all segments and seasons.

Instead, pricing decisions should be adapted according to:

- Season
- Segment sensitivity
- Demand pressure
- Booking pace
- Cancellation risk
- Inventory availability

### Practical RM Action

In periods or segments with stronger pricing power, the hotel could increase ADR more confidently.

In more price-sensitive segments or low-demand periods, the hotel should be more careful with rate increases and may need to combine pricing with commercial actions.

---

## 5. Inventory Protection Strategy

### Business Insight

Inventory is limited, and not all bookings have the same revenue value or risk level.

Accepting too much low-yield or high-risk demand too early can reduce the hotel’s ability to capture higher-value bookings later.

### Revenue Management Recommendation

The hotel should protect inventory during high-demand periods by considering both revenue value and cancellation risk.

This means looking beyond occupancy and asking:

- Which demand should be accepted?
- Which demand should be restricted?
- Which segments should receive more availability?
- Which channels create more risk?
- Which dates require stronger controls?

### Practical RM Action

For high-demand dates, the hotel could apply minimum length of stay controls, close lower-yield rates, reduce availability for weaker segments or prioritize more profitable demand.

---

## 6. Final Revenue Management Recommendations

Based on the full case study, the main recommendations are:

1. Analyze segment performance using revenue, ADR and room nights together, not only booking volume.
2. Prioritize high-value segments during periods of strong demand.
3. Monitor cancellation risk by segment and channel.
4. Use cancellation behavior to improve net demand forecasts and overbooking assumptions.
5. Forecast demand by month and segment to support pricing and inventory decisions.
6. Avoid applying one general pricing rule to all segments.
7. Interpret price elasticity carefully, considering seasonality and demand pressure.
8. Protect inventory during high-demand periods from low-yield or high-risk demand.
9. Connect technical analysis with practical Revenue Management actions.
10. Use the findings as decision support, not as automatic pricing rules.

---

## 7. Limitations of the Analysis

This project is based on historical booking data and should be interpreted as a Revenue Management case study, not as a complete commercial strategy.

Some important limitations include:

- The dataset does not include competitor pricing.
- The dataset does not include real-time market demand.
- The dataset does not include full profitability or cost information.
- Price elasticity results may be affected by seasonality and demand-driven pricing.
- Forecasting models should be validated with more recent business data before operational use.

These limitations are important because real Revenue Management decisions require both data analysis and business context.

---

## 8. Final Business Conclusion

The main conclusion of this case study is that hotel Revenue Management decisions should be based on a combination of demand, segmentation, cancellation behavior, pricing sensitivity and inventory strategy.

The strongest value of the analysis is not only in calculating metrics, but in using those metrics to support better commercial decisions.

This project demonstrates how hotel booking data can be transformed into practical Revenue Management insights that support pricing, forecasting, channel mix and inventory decisions.
